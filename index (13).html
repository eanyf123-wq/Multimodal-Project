What is optimization culture & where did it come from?

Productivity culture didn't rise on Instagram. In fact, it goes back centuries…

Click on each era to explore
The Origins of Optimization Culture
Puritan Roots
In his 1905 book, sociologist Max Weber argued that Calvinist religion contributed to the development of capitalism due to its values of diligence, discipline, and prudence. Hard work wasn't perceived as purely practical but rather, as evidence of moral worth.
1905
Puritan Roots
Post WWII Shift
The post-war consumer economy led to mistaken beliefs that work is synonymous with productivity and that success is determined mostly by accumulating wealth.
1950s

Silicon Valley Goes Mainstream
Stories of tech founders pulling all-nighters and beating the competition became popular and legendary spreading the "rise and grind" mentality across social media.

What is burnout?
And what is it not?

The term "burnout" is used to describe a state of severe fatigue caused by chronic stress, typically linked to work.

Burnout

🪫
Emotional Exhaustion
Feeling completely drained, emotionally and physically. There is nothing left to give.
🧊
Cynicism
A growing detachment and indifference toward your studies, goals, and the people around you.
📉
Reduced Personal Efficacy
A collapse in your sense of competence. Feeling like nothing you do makes a difference anymore.
Burnout
What burnout is not.

Burnout is often inaccurately confused with these things. It is important to be identified and acknowledged as a global academic and occupational phenomenon. Not a personal flaw.

The data
How widespread is burnout among college students?
Across studies, burnout affected between 38% and over 60% of university students.

Source: Systematic review, PMC (peer-reviewed)

More than half of college students experience emotional exhaustion, caused by high workload, curriculum demands, and prolonged study hours.

It is systemic,
not personal.

Unfortunately burnout is not personal failure. It is the system's responsibility. It's the system that fails the students, not the other way around.

Social media aggravates this since it does not responsibly reflect the optimization culture. It makes it visible and comparative at all times. Moreover, social media use and digital distraction worsen burnout: increasing mental fatigue, reducing concentration, and disrupting sleep.

Whose fault is it?
Who is responsible?

This mental health and burnout crisis is not a personal flaw of students. It is a sign that the academic and educational system demands more than it supports. From the early 2000s onwards, research highlighted that first-year and minority students were affected more significantly by mental health issues due to social isolation, financial strains, and lack of culturally sensitive resources.

So what does healthy optimization look like?
The goal is not to stop caring.
It's to stop treating yourself
like a robot.

Students should understand the difference between optimizing for performance and optimizing for well-being.

Conclusion
All the college students, including myself, should realize: we are not productivity machines.

We are not our GPA, our internship, or our habit streak. Rest is not a reward to be earned. It is a human right.
